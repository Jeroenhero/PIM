Introduction

Over the past four weeks I have attended the course Project Interactive Multimedia (PIM).
My two classmates, Jeroen Braakman and Stan Eveleens, and I have made RijksRoute:
a webapp which allows users to either generate or pick a route through the rijksmusem best suited for them.
This portfolio contains my feedback on all the lectures we had, the trip to the rijksmuseum and more.

Lectures

Lecture Opinion
Lecture 1: Introduction: Program or be programmed This lecture gave a good idea on what this course was going to be about in my opinion. My team and I already had a clear vision on what we wanted to do, and this lecture showed us how to organize and structurize our plan.
Lecture 2: Technical Setup The info on firebase, Git and other resources were a bit redundant for us since we've already worked with them, but the lecture was overall well structurized and allowed us to conclude that we had everything set up properly
Trip: Rijksmuseum Visit The pitches given by employees of the Rijksmuseum were very useful, as they only made us feel more secure about our idea and gave us a glimpse of how to properly pitch ourselves. Some subjects were more interesting than others which sometimes caused me to loose my focus, but in the end I learned a lot of interseting things I never thought I would. Such as the p;rocess of restaurating a painting, or how complicated a ticket system can be. The tour through the museum was exremely insightful and above all fun. Walking thorugh the museum with a guide who no only gave us the best route, but who was also a walking encyclopedia, regarding the artpieces, made the tour really enjoyable. This made me think even more about possible routes through the museum.
Lecture 3: Web apps for Museums and UX basics The parts about UX degsin were especially interesting, since it showed me how much it affects how users use your website. During the time before the lecture we had already drawn a sketch of our website, and at the end of the lecture we had to do this again. The feedback from other students as well as our professor was really helpful as it was the foundation of our site's current look.
Presentations: Pitches 1 It was kind of difficult to put everything we wanted to do and accomplish in a short 2-minute pitch, but in the end it worked out quite well. It was also nice to know that no other group had a really similar idea, which made our project more unique than anticipated.
Lecture 4: How to pitch your idea After this lecture the main idea on how to porperly pitch was way clearer. Focus on real user stories and don't talk too much about technical details. People want to see short, clear and enthusiastic pitches, not tech tlak with Sebastiaan!
Presentations: Pitches 2 We scored a 3/4 so we were very content with how it went. We were able to show everyone in the classroom how our website was going to be built structure wise, and most important aspects were already functioning.
Lecture 5: Organizational Context This guest lecture was given bij Inge van der Weerd, and she explained a lot about information systems and their role in the rijksmuseum. Furthermore she explained how those IS are implemented, and that our IS has to be unique for the rijksmuseum. She also explained how difficult it is to proerply implement an IS, so I gained a lot in insight from this lecture
Lecture 6: Accurator: nichesourcing Chrijs Dijkhoorn gave this lecture, he works at the rijksmuseum and is quite experienced in the field we're working. It was really interesting to see that someone who studied artificial intelligence at the VU some years ago, ended up in his position at the rijksmusem. This showed me that IMM has even more career oppurtunities than anticipated!
Final presentation We were more than well prepared, and really enjoyed the 'market' idea in which we could demonstrate our project. We ended up winning the public's vote on best web app and were top four in the Rijksmuseum's opinion! It was really interesting to see how other groups had done their projects and what ideas they had.

Read literature

To be honest I haven't read much literature for this course. I've read some documentation on w3schools about certain css and html properties like the darkening effect when you hover over the two pictures on our homepage, and some lay-out instructions for Bootstrap. There weren't any mandatory books for the course, so I mainly did everything by trial and error!

Self reflection

I believe it's safe to say that I had an active, participating role in this project. From the creation of the idea, up to the demo presentation of our website I have always contributed my part to the project. Jeroen and Stan did a great job themselves as well, this project couldn't have been what is is today without either one of us three. We divided the tasks evenly, whether it was coding in html, css, and javascript, or designing logo's, communicating with employees from the Rijksmuseum, and more. I personally mainly designed the website itself, and coded the route generator page. But we all helped each other with each other's tasks at some point.

This course has given me a first taste of what I might do after my study. It combined many aspects from previous courses into one project in which we were free to do almost everything the way we wanted to. I think it was a really good move to make this course about the rijksmuseum, it gave us the opportunity to put our knowledge to the test and try to use everything we've learned this past year, to create something that might actually have an effect on many people. Above that, my website building skills have definitely improved in ways not foreseen! I really got the hang of Bootstrap, and with help from Jeroen I was able to learn a lot about both javascript and Dom, which is really useful.

I've gained quite some additional insights, whether it's how to pitch an idea in an insanely short amount of time, or how complicated some simple looking information systems actually are. Above all I gained the insight to use many different types of knowledge to create an end product like RijksRoute.
